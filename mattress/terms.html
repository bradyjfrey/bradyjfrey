Mattress Terms

Common terms and definitions for mattress shopping. Source: Mattress Makers (Gabe & Pablo), May 2024.

Construction

Hybrid Mattress
A mattress that combines pocketed coils as the support layer with a specialty foam on top (usually memory foam or natural latex). Hybrids tend to have a smooth top rather than a quilted top, with one or two layers plus a cover.
Pocket Coils (Wrapped/Encased Coils)
Each coil is individually wrapped in its own fabric pocket, allowing independent movement. Not all pocket coil systems are equal; a four-pack configuration (like the Texas pocket spring) is more stable and supportive than a single-line arrangement, which can lean and create an accordion effect.
Power Loaded Coils (Power Packed Coils)
A coil that's pre-compressed into a shorter pocket than its natural length (e.g., a 10-inch coil in an 8-inch casing). The coil constantly pushes back, providing more tension and support. Helps prevent body impressions and dipping over time.
Foam Encasement
A 3-4 inch border of stiff foam around the entire perimeter of the coil system. Gives a firm edge and a finished look, but it's cheaper to manufacture than edge-to-edge coils. Lower-density foam encasement breaks down faster over time.
Zoning
Different firmness levels in different areas of the mattress (e.g., softer at the shoulders, firmer in the middle, softer at the feet). Can be done with coils or foam. Zones are fixed, but people's bodies are different sizes, so couples especially will hit the zones in different spots. Custom zoning works but is expensive.
Tufting / Inner Tufting
A technique where a needle and thread are pulled through the mattress layers, secured with fabric buttons, to hold everything together without glue. Reduces body impressions and shifting. Downside: some people can feel the button indentations. Inner tufting is the same process but on the interior, so it's not visible on the surface.
Tape Edge
The piping or binding sewn around the perimeter of the mattress to close it off. It's how the top and bottom panels are joined. Not every mattress uses one (some use zippers). Not an essential feature, but knowing the term signals to a salesperson that you've done your homework.

Materials

ILD (Indentation Load Deflection)
A firmness rating. It measures how many pounds of pressure it takes to compress a 4-inch block of material by 25% (1 inch). Higher ILD = firmer; lower ILD = softer. A 25 ILD means it takes ~24 lbs of pressure to compress it 1 inch.
Dunlop vs. Talalay Latex
Two manufacturing processes for latex foam, both sourced from rubber trees. Dunlop is less expensive; Talalay costs more due to additional processing steps. Even at the same ILD, they can feel different. Both come in synthetic, blended, and natural varieties.
Convoluted Foam (Egg Crate Foam)
Foam that's been machine-cut into an egg crate shape. Manufacturers take a 3-inch block of foam, cut it in half, and get two pieces out of it; you're getting half the material. Often marketed as "air-rated" or more breathable, but it's mostly a cost-saving move, not a meaningful feature.
Mattress Ticking
The fabric cover on the outside of the mattress. The ticking affects feel, responsiveness, and breathability. Changing the ticking alone can change how a mattress feels, even with the same internals. Three main types:
  • Damask: Tighter woven weave, no stretch. Most breathable of the common types. Creates a firmer, more structured feel. Traditional luxury choice.
  • Knit: Extremely soft, stretchy, flat surface. Usually polyester synthetic blend. Most common on modern mattresses, especially bed-in-a-box. Mainly used as a covering for memory foam or latex topped mattresses.
  • Jacquard: Similar to damask in feel (structured, minimal stretch, stiffer) but with more complex woven patterns. Jacquard refers to a loom technique allowing individual thread control; the weave structure determines actual performance, not the pattern complexity.

Performance

Motion Separation
How well a mattress isolates movement so one partner doesn't disturb the other. Best performers: memory foam, latex, and pocketed coils. Worst: connected/bonnell coil spring systems.
Coil Density vs. Coil Count
Coil density is an inflated number representing how many coils would fit if the foam encasement weren't there. Coil count is the actual number of coils inside the mattress. Coil density is essentially a padded stat; coil count is the real number.

Warranty

Pro-rated vs. Non-pro-rated Warranty
Non-pro-rated means the company covers 100% of replacement for the full warranty period. Pro-rated means full coverage for part of the period, then decreasing coverage (you pay a percentage) for the remainder. A 20-year warranty that's 10/10 pro-rated means full coverage for 10 years, partial for the next 10. Don't be impressed by long warranty lengths without reading the fine print.
